# Chapter Three — The Unburied Vale

The Vale announced itself, from a long way off, as a smell.

Not the wet-earth smell of a graveyard — that would have been honest, and Elin was learning that the honest horrors were the ones you could bear. This was the smell of a graveyard that had never been allowed to *finish*: the smell of grief kept at exactly the temperature where it would neither cool nor combust, for sixty years, until the whole valley had cured in it like a ham.

The Ember in her chest, which had spent the road down from the camps getting quieter and quieter, went very warm at the gate.

*Home*, it said, and she wished it hadn't.

There was a woman waiting under the arch of the Vigil Gate, in the grey habit of the Choir but wearing it wrong somehow — wearing it the way you wear a coat you mean to give away. She had a hard, tired, kind face, and she looked at Elin with the specific relief of someone who has been waiting for a weapon and is trying not to be glad the weapon has a person attached.

"You're the bearer Maren wrote ahead about," she said. "Good. Ilse. Cantor, once — I sang for this congregation thirty years before I heard what I was singing." She fell in beside Elin without asking, which Elin was coming to recognize as the mark of the useful. "I left the Choir the day they voted to keep a dying man alive as furniture. I'll explain on the way to that sentence making sense. Come and see what my faith built."

What the faith had built was this: the Choir did not bury its dead.

The rot, Ilse explained as they walked in under a sky the colour of an old wound, was the land's one honest truth. Everything decayed; everything was leaving all the time; the Choir had simply stopped calling that bad news. And so the dead were not put in the ground — they *walked their own funerals*, forever, in a slow eternal procession from the gate to the cathedral at the valley's crown. Sixty years of forever, now. Fourteen thousand two hundred and six of them, the last honest count Elin would hear, gate to cathedral, every open grave in the Vale holding a tenant that would not lie still.

"And at the top of it," Ilse said, "kneels Saint Varo. The one man in Vaelscar the rot refuses. His flesh will not decay — cannot, they say — and so my congregation worships his misery and calls it *proof*. Proof that the rot *chooses*, you see. That it's holy, and not just weather." She stopped, at the edge of the Misted Fields, and looked at Elin with her hard tired eyes. "Three stand between you and him. The Sexton, who digs and digs and never fills. Vess, the first widow they ever told *no* — her scream became our liturgy, and I sang it for thirty years before I heard the words. And Varo himself, who has been begging to die longer than you've been alive."

"And you want him dead," Elin said.

"I want him *released*." Ilse's jaw tightened. "The Choir calls that murder. I've stopped arguing the point. Whichever word you use, the man on his knees up there has asked for it every day for sixty years, and I am the only one who ever thought his asking was worth more than my faith's need to keep him kneeling."

The Ember, warm and at home, offered its opinion. *A saint the rot won't take, and you carrying a splinter of the very thing that won't take him. Do you see it? You could* finish *him where the rot can't. He'd thank you. She'd thank you. Everyone thanks the healer who's willing to go one step further than the squeamish.*

"Gently," Elin said aloud — to Ilse, and to the thing in her chest. "If the fight allows it, I'll give him what he's asking for, and I'll give it gently."

Ilse looked at her for a long moment. "Thirty years in the Choir," she said, "and I never once heard the word *gently* aimed at Varo. Go east, bearer. The Sexton holds the fields. No one reaches the cathedral while he still has holes to offer."

---

Old Fenna caught her at the field's edge, before the fighting started.

She was a small woman with a knot of grey hair and hands that would not stop working at a scrap of cloth. "My son walks the Misted Fields," she said, without preamble, the way people said things when they'd said them a thousand times and stopped expecting to be heard. "Fourth from the alder, grey coat. The Choir says that's him *honored*. I say I sewed that coat for a living boy and I want it *back* on a dead one, in the ground, where coats and sons go."

Elin looked out at the fields, where the mist moved and, in the mist, things that had been people moved with it.

"If it's my hand that fells him," she said, "he falls facing home. Fourth from the alder, grey coat. I'll remember."

Fenna's hands went still. "Facing home," she repeated. "Yes." Something in her face, some long-clenched thing, loosened by a single turn. "The Choir sang at me for sixty years and never once said anything that useful."

The Sexton was waiting in the middle of the fields, and he had known she was coming, because knowing was his whole terrible trade.

"I dug yours the day you crossed the gate," he told her. He was a big man, or had been; the grave-dirt had gotten into him the way rot got into wood, and he leaned on a spade worn thin with sixty years of a job that never ended. "Third row, by the alder. Dry ground — you'll keep." And then the earth between the graves began to move, because he did not fight with his hands alone; the Vale answered him, the unburied clawing up out of the soil wherever he pointed, and Elin learned, that day, the arithmetic of him: that his threat was not strength but *addition*, an endless patient sum of small dead things, and that the only way through was to keep her feet moving and her head clear and refuse to be *drowned*.

She noticed, fighting him, that the dead he raised came apart when they fell near each other — a grim chain, a corpse taking its neighbour with it — and that he walked toward the clusters on purpose, to make the floor of the world betray her. She spread her kills wide. She kept the alder at her back. And when she finally reached him through the churned and steaming ground and put him down, she did it near the fourth grave, so that the boy in the grey coat — she found him, she checked — went into the dark facing west, facing home, facing the door of a house that a woman with a knot of grey hair had kept lit for sixty years on the chance.

The Sexton folded into the soil like a man getting into bed at the end of a very long day. Behind him, for the first time in sixty years, a grave in the Vale stood *closed*. The road east sang about it — high, thin, and furious.

---

She found the First Grave in a side-aisle of the Vale, and it nearly had her.

It was the original one — the grave the Choir had dug and then refused to fill, sixty years ago, the day their faith was born. Sixty years open; the sides had gone smooth as a font. And it was full of *offerings* — sixty years of them, worked down into the soil. Rings. Clasps. A child's silver whistle, exactly like the one on a founder's plinth in a keep she'd never see.

The Ember leaned over the edge with her, and it was not subtle.

*Look at all that. The dead aren't using it. Sixty years of grief, right there, and it would come up easily — I can feel how easily. You're going into a chapter of fights against things that heal and things that swarm. You'll want every advantage. Reach down.*

Elin knelt at the edge of the smooth-sided grave, and she did not reach down. Instead she said a name aloud — the first patient she had ever lost, a girl younger than Perrin, a fever she'd been too slow and too green to catch, years before the green light ever came. She said the name once, quietly, and let the grave hold it.

The grave took the name the way dry ground takes rain, and the Ember in her chest went still — the specific stillness it only ever had when a thing was *paid* for and not *taken*. She walked away lighter by exactly one name's weight, and she could still remember the girl; she checked. That was the strange part, the part she'd think about for miles. It cost her nothing she wasn't glad to spend, and it left the thing in her chest quiet and, she thought, faintly cheated.

---

Vess the Unburied held the Silent Aisle, and Vess was the first one who made Elin want to lose.

"They sang *no* over my husband's grave," the widow said. She was a ruin of a woman, translucent with sixty years of one unbroken scream, and her voice came from everywhere in the long aisle at once. "Sixty years I have sung it back at them. Do you know what it *costs*, to be a liturgy? Stand still, little bearer. The silence in this aisle is *mine*, and I decide who gets to rest in it."

The fight was a fight against grief that had learned geometry. When Vess wailed, the whole aisle screamed with her — and there was exactly one circle of quiet in it, one place the sound did not reach, and Elin had to find that place and stand in it while the world came apart around the edges. She learned to read the widow's grief the way she'd learned to read a fever: where it would break, where the quiet would be, how long she had. It was, she thought, the most intimate fight of her life — standing in the one silent place a dead woman still owned, waiting out the noise of the worst thing that had ever happened to someone.

She won it because grief, even sixty years of it, has an end, and Elin had learned in a dozen sickrooms how to wait for the end of a thing without flinching from it.

The scream did not fade. It *ended* — the way a debt ends. And what settled over the aisle afterward was not silence, which is empty, but *quiet*, which is full, and which the Vale had not heard in sixty years.

Up the hill, the cathedral bells began to toll. The congregation knew she was coming.

---

They were kneeling in a field between her and the cathedral doors. Hundreds of them, unarmed, in the Choir's grey, and they did not move.

An old cantor rose from the front row with his hands open. "You've come to take our saint," he said. "We know. We heard the bells count you up the hill." His voice shook, not with anger. "Please. He is all the proof we have that the rot *chooses*. Without him, our sixty years of grief were just... grief." He said it like a man naming the thing he was most afraid was true. "Please, bearer. Which is it? Were we chosen? Or were we only sad?"

The Ember had a great deal to say about this. It liked a congregation. It especially liked, she was learning, a congregation of Mórwyn's faithful, kneeling, asking a bearer of Mórwyn's own fire to decide the meaning of their suffering. *Tell them anything*, it purred. *You could tell them he's a wick — that he feeds the thing that will eat them all — and half of them would harden and hate you and the other half would break, and either way you'd walk through them in seconds. Or tell them he's holy. Or say nothing. They'll make a legend of your silence. You could leave here a saint yourself. I've done it before.*

Elin looked at the kneeling field — at the young faces and the old ones, at the grief that had waited sixty years for permission to be nothing but grief — and she told them the truth, because it was the only thing she had that the Ember hadn't handed her.

"Your saint has begged sixty years to die," she said. Her voice carried further than she meant it to; the field was very quiet. "I'm not taking him from you. I'm returning him to himself."

The old cantor's mouth worked. "Returning him—" He stopped.

And somewhere in the rows behind him, one voice — young, cracked, a girl's — said, into the silence: "...he *does* scream at night. We all hear it. We *sing* over it."

The kneeling field was very quiet as it opened her a lane. They made it themselves, without being told. And walking up it, between hundreds of grieving people who had just been given, by a stranger carrying a splinter of their god, permission to grieve, Elin understood something she would carry the rest of the way: that grief had been waiting sixty years down here for someone to say it was allowed to just be grief. That the Choir's whole madness was a room full of people who had never once been permitted to put the thing down.

She thought of Fenna. Facing home. She kept walking.

---

Saint Varo was on his knees in the cathedral, where he had been for sixty years, and he was weeping before she said a word.

"Sixty years I have knelt here," he said, "asking the rot to take me, and it will not. My flesh *refuses* the one honest thing in Vaelscar." He was enormous and unrotting and unbearably, patiently sad. "The Choir built a cathedral around my failure and calls it holiness. I am done asking politely. Come, bearer. Be my answer."

And here was the cruelty of it, the specific cruelty the Vale had saved for last: the thing that would not take him was the thing Elin carried in her chest. Mórwyn's fire. The rot. He had spent sixty years begging the decay to have him, and the decay had a splinter in the room now, in the body of a village healer, and it *still would not*, because the splinter was hers, and she would not let it.

They fought in the round cathedral, and it was the slowest, heaviest fight of the Vale, and it was made of two things. Censer-bearers rang the arena — stationary faithful swinging incense that *healed* him, that fed the rot he could not have, so that Elin had to cut the censers down before the man himself would even feel her, the DPS turned inside out, the mercy she'd promised held hostage by his own congregation's devotion. And the great bell — the Toll — that rang out a killing wave through the whole cathedral except the shadows of the standing pillars, so that she learned, again, the lesson of the Vale: find the one place the noise can't reach, and be there when it lands.

At a quarter of his strength, he did the thing the Choir had built a cathedral to prevent. He *stood up*. First time in sixty years. He came off his knees, and the whole cathedral seemed to flinch, and he wept the entire time, and moved half again as fast, and the tolls came faster, and Elin — dodging, ducking behind the cracking pillars, keeping her feet — found that she was weeping too, a little, which the Ember found incomprehensible and said so.

She gave him what he'd asked for. Gently, as gently as a fight that size allowed. And Saint Varo did not get up again. The rot took him at last — gently, from the inside, like a door unlocking from within — and his face, at the very end, was nothing in the world but *grateful*.

*You could have kept the moment*, the Ember said, quietly, as the great body went still and, for the first time in sixty years, began — slowly, decently — to change. *He was proof of something, you know. Proof the rot chooses. And it does. Look at him going. It chose. You just made it choose.* A warmth, almost coaxing. *You're better at this than the healing, aren't you. Ending. It's cleaner. There's no mark left over.*

"There's always a mark," Elin said. She thought of a grey stain over a seven-year-old's heart, still spreading, a promise she had not begun to keep. "That's the difference between us. I keep count of them. You just like the count going down."

The Ember went quiet. Home was not, she was beginning to suspect, as comfortable for it as it had hoped.

---

Ilse found her on the cathedral steps, in the first quiet the Vale had known in sixty years.

"So the saint gets his grave," the ex-cantor said, "and the Choir loses its shame and its shrine in one blow." She looked out over the valley, where the endless procession had, here and there, begun to *stop*. Where a gravedigger she'd met on the road — an old man who'd kept his spade oiled through sixty years of madness, against exactly this day — was already at work, filling the first holes. "They will not forgive that. Neither will they forget who freed him." She glanced at Elin. "That's you, bearer. For good and ill, that's you. The Vale will sing about the day the funeral ended, and the song will not be sure whether you were a mercy or a thief, and that's the truest song they could write, so let them."

Below, someone was putting a boy in a grey coat into the ground, facing home. An old woman with a knot of grey hair stood over the grave and did not weep, exactly. She looked, for the first time in sixty years, like a woman who could go home herself.

"South," Ilse said, and nodded at the horizon, where the sky glowed the wrong colour for sunset. "The foundries of the Slagfields are running day and night now. And something under them has started answering the hammers." She looked at Elin with her hard, tired, kind face. "You'll want to sleep first. You won't, but you'll want to. Go carefully, bearer. And — thank you. For the *gently*. Thirty years I waited to hear someone mean it."

Elin went south, toward the wrong-coloured light, with the Vale finally burying its dead behind her and a promise still unkept in her chest — *I will find how, and I will undo it* — and a voice in that same chest that had gone home to the country of the rot and found, to its evident surprise, that the healer it had chosen was harder to be at home in than it had counted on.
