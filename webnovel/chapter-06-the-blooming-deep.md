# Chapter Six — The Blooming Deep

The bog should have stunk of rot, and it stank of clover, and that was the first thing that was wrong.

Elin had spent a whole chapter of her life learning the honest smell of decay — had walked the Unburied Vale gate to cathedral in it. This was decay's *opposite*, and it was worse, because decay at least kept its promises. Here the drowned trees fruited. Here the reeds bloomed out of season. Here, deep in the Blooming Deep, the blight that killed everything had a twin that had learned to make things *grow* — wrong, and huge, and permanently — and the terrain itself was the story: bog and spore, death and its green mockery, interleaved room by room until you couldn't tell which you were standing in.

The Ember in her chest recognized the country the way it had recognized the Vale, and hated recognizing it.

*Cousins again*, it said, low. *The Vale was mine — rot, the honest ending that isn't really an ending. This is the Root's, and it's the same trick pointed the other way. It doesn't refuse endings by denying them. It refuses them by* never letting anything stop growing. *Add and add and add, and call it life.* A pause; something almost like fear, which Elin had never heard from it. *Be careful here, bearer. This is the one place in Vaelscar that could actually get rid of me. And I find — this surprises us both — that I would rather you didn't.*

The woman at the Pilgrim Gate wore the Choir's grey, and wore it like a person who had watched her whole faith walk into the one thing it couldn't digest. Deacon Vela. "A shard-bearer, at *our* gate," she said. "The flame has a sense of humour after all. Out here, deacon mostly means the one who keeps the count when people don't come back." She looked Elin over, and something in her softened. "You're the bearer from the ice — the one who hauled a wagon of sleepers back to the fires. My faithful argued about you for three nights. Half called it desecration of a vow. The other half quietly wished someone had hauled *their* kin south." She almost smiled. "I was in the second half. Welcome to a worse question than the ice asked you."

The worse question was this. The Choir had come to the Deep on pilgrimage, expecting holy rot — the land telling its one honest truth. Sixty years that creed had held, through plague and Vale and worse. And then they walked into the Deep and found the *opposite* of rot: growth without death. Things that only added. And half of Vela's flock had knelt to it on sight, because a faith that flips once flips easy, and because — Elin was beginning to understand — grief will convert to whatever promises to stop.

"Three stand between you and the heart of it," Vela said. "The Auroch, in the wallow — the bog-bull that drowned a century back and never stopped growing. Rotmaw, past the canal — my deacon, once. Gardens for the lie now." Her voice caught. "And in the heart of the Bloom, the Wildfang shaman Kaethra, who drank the green looking for a cure. Both her camps agree she cannot leave the Deep. And bearer — when Wildfang camps agree, the thing they agree on is always terrible."

"I'll hear her out," Elin said, "before anything ends. She's owed that much."

Vela looked at her a long moment. "You know what the Deep has taught me?" she said. "Every creed I own fits a funeral. *None* of them fit a woman who cured herself into something worse and stayed kind through it. Hear her out. And come tell an old deacon what you heard. My theology needs the bruise."

---

Herbalist Kesh met her at the cure-camp, and Kesh had sent the runner that brought her, and so the weight of it started with Kesh, and Kesh knew it.

"Kaethra was my teacher," she said. "My chief, and the best of us by a margin I won't insult with modesty. Our whole people is one argument, bearer: cure the beast-blood, or accept it. Kaethra led the cure camp. And she was *honest* — that's the wound of it. She wouldn't test the Root's green on a child, or a volunteer, or an enemy. Herself only." Kesh's hands were steady in the way that costs something. "It worked. The beast in her blood is gone. And the Root moved into the empty room. Now the hunt-camp says *we told you so* and weeps while saying it."

*The beast is gone*, the Ember murmured, and it was doing arithmetic Elin didn't like. *Read that the way she means it, bearer. The cure* works. *The thing you've carried since Millrow — you could have it gone. The mark on the boy. The voice in your chest. All of it, cured. It's real. It's in the water not two miles from here.*

"When it's done," Elin told Kesh, "I'll carry her answer back to both camps. Whatever it turns out to be."

Kesh's breath went out of her. "Her *answer*," she said. "Yes. That's the first framing of this that hasn't made me want to break something. She always said the argument would end with evidence, not victory. She just never meant to *be* it." She gripped Elin's arm. "Go. And if any part of her is still choosing words in there — let her finish them."

A fisher at the gate, an old man named Dov, told Elin the thing that stayed with her longest, longer than the monsters. "Thirty years I've fished this bog," he said. "It takes. That's its nature — a net a season, a cousin a decade. I made my peace with a taking bog. Then last spring it started giving *back*." He looked out at the water. "My old dog, dead four winters, scratching at my door one morning, looking *exactly right*. That's when I moved to the gate. Exactly right is how you know." He would not meet her eyes. "I still leave the door unlatched, nights. He sits past the firelight, looking exactly right. Hardest thing I do, most nights, is not calling him in. *Free* is the most expensive price, bearer. My gran said that. Thirty years fishing and the spring the bog turned generous is the spring I lost my nerve."

---

The Auroch was almost a mercy, because it was only a monster.

It was a bog-bull the size of a granary that had drowned a hundred years ago and taken it as advice — the Root got into it the way rings get into a tree — and it was not angry, had not been anything in a century. It was a weather system with horns, and Elin was standing in its season. It sank and surfaced and chased her under the water with lines of erupting bog; she kept moving, or tanked it, and read the terrain, and put it back under for good.

When it settled into the wallow one last time, the water went mirror-still, and in it she saw the wrongness plainly: every reed at the waterline was *blooming*, out of season, out of sense. And deeper in, someone had planted the poison in rows. Rows meant a gardener.

Rotmaw the Gardener had been Vela's deacon once, and had come to the Deep and converted on the spot, and gardened now. "I preached rot for thirty years," he told her, tending his carnivorous beds, his converts drifting among the blooms like the faithful they'd been a season ago. "The land's one honest truth. Then I saw the Blooming, and understood: rot is only the truth *sleeping*. This garden is what it dreams." He smiled, mild and lethal. "Kneel and I'll show you the roots. One way or another."

She fought him in a garden that filled itself faster than she could weed it, and won the way you win against a thing that heals when you feed it its own dead — carefully, from the outside in — and when he folded into his own beds the garden began, very slowly, deprived of its gardener, to *eat itself*. Even his converts stood baffled; the blooms they'd worshipped that morning were compost by dusk. Faith that flips once flips easy. Faith that flips twice just breaks.

From the deep grove came drumbeats. Three beats, then silence. The cure-camp's summons rhythm — the pattern that meant *come and see*, and also meant *we are so sorry*.

---

She found the Cure Pool before she found Kaethra, and the Cure Pool was the fight of her life, and nothing swam up out of it to touch her.

It was the spring Kaethra had drunk from. Green water, utterly clear, and under it white roots in slow motion. The camp had fenced it round with bone stakes and warnings in three scripts, and the fence was leaning *inward* now, pulled gently, month by month.

The water knew what she carried. It was already offering.

*The Ember's whisper — gone quiet.* She could feel the offer land like a hand on a fever. *The debt lifted. The temptation cured. Growth without death. And the mark, bearer — the grey mark, over a seven-year-old's heart, still spreading in a village a whole country north of here. You promised to undo it. You have carried "I will find how" for a hundred miles and you have not found how, because there* is *no how, not for you, not with your two failing mortal hands.* The water was so clear. *There's a how in here. One mouthful, and you'll go home and put your hands on that boy and the grey will* recede *— growth, undoing, the Root never subtracts, it only adds back what was taken. You could keep the promise. You could keep* every *promise. All it has ever asked is room.*

And Elin stood at the leaning fence, and this was the temptation the whole long road had been building toward, aimed with perfect knowledge at the two wounds she'd carried since the first night in Hesta's doorway: *silence the voice*, and *undo the harm.* Everything she wanted. The cure was real. Kesh had said so. Kaethra had *proven* it, on a market day, in front of her whole camp, because evidence belonged to everyone.

The Ember in her chest had gone completely silent. Not tempting. *Afraid* — the specific held-breath silence of a thing that knows it cannot argue its own case, because arguing *keep me* here would mean arguing for its own leash, and it was too proud for that, and too frightened to be quiet. For the first time since Millrow, Elin was alone with the choice. The voice that had offered her every shortcut for a hundred miles had nothing to say, because for once the shortcut led somewhere it did not want to go.

She thought of Kaethra, who had been wiser than her and twice as careful, and who was, right now, two miles away, *worn* — the cure working, and the bottle currently *wearing her*. She thought of the dog past Dov's firelight, looking exactly right. She thought of Aldric by his cold fire: *being chosen is not the same as being owned. You pick what the reason means.*

She knelt at the leaning fence, and she did not drink.

Instead she spoke her temptation aloud to the open water — named the exact thing the Ember wanted of her, the thing she had carried since her shard woke: *to perfect them whatever it costs. To stop being squeamish about the method. To make the not-knowing end.* She said it plainly, to the clear green water, and spoken aloud it sounded *smaller*. Ownable. A thing with edges, that belonged to her, that she could hold in one hand and refuse.

"I'm keeping it," she told the water. "The voice, and the mark, and the not-knowing. They're mine. That's the only cure I'll take — the one where I get to keep the thing and stay the one who decides what it means." Her own voice was steady, which surprised her. "You can undo the harm, maybe. I believe you. But you'd have to undo *me* to do it, one mouthful at a time, adding and adding until there was nothing left that a seven-year-old's mother would recognize. Kaethra found that out. I'm not going to make her lesson wasted."

The pool's stillness flickered, once. An offer had never been declined by *naming* before; somewhere among the white roots something filed her under DIFFICULT. And the Ember in her chest let out a breath it did not have — spared, changed, bound to her now in a way it had not chosen and did not fully understand, the way a thing is bound to the person who had the power to end it and didn't.

*You could have been free of me*, it said, quietly, as she walked away.

"So could you of me," Elin said. "Neither of us drank. That's going to have to be the whole of what we trust about each other."

She reset the leaning fence-stakes on her way out — drove them deep, angled them out — because a fence had never once held the water and both of them knew it, but a fence was a *sentence*, written in bone, that said *somebody decided no*, and travelers would read it. It was honest work, the kind hands remember. The water watched with what she'd have sworn was amusement. She left it certain she'd be back, and she left it wrong.

---

Kaethra was lucid the whole time, and that was the thing that would not leave Elin afterward. Not the fight. The *lucidity*.

"Stop," the shaman said, when Elin reached the heart of the Bloom. "Before anything happens, you will hear it from *me*, plainly, while the words are still mine to choose: the cure works. The beast in my blood is gone. Gone, bearer. I checked for a month before I let myself believe it." She stood in a grove that was her and was not her, roots running out of her into the peat. "Then I checked what was holding the door where the beast had been." A breath. "Both camps sent for you, didn't they. First thing they've agreed on in a generation. Come, then. The Root is very interested in meeting you, and I can't hold its arm much longer."

They fought, and the fight *was* the cutscene, because Kaethra narrated it. She swapped forms as her strength failed — the huntress, charging, Whitepelt's own lineage, alone in a way that should be felt; and the bloom, rooted in place, healing while her roots lived, raining spore-volleys and speaking the whole time in her own steady voice. She was fighting the Root as hard as she was fighting Elin, holding its arm, spending her lucidity like coin, and each swap she said one true thing, and Elin cut the roots when she had to and hated every cut, because the woman inside them had, by every account, been the best of a good people, and had done the one honest thing, and this was the price of asking honestly.

At the very end — when there was almost nothing left of her, and the fight could have gone one more brutal round — the Root *let go*.

"...There," Kaethra said, and her voice was wholly hers. "Do you feel it? It *let go* of me. The Root doesn't fight ruins, and that's what's left to fight over. So. These last words are mine, wholly mine, and I get to spend them: the cure was real. Tell them that. And the price was real. Tell them *both*, or tell them nothing." She looked at Elin, unafraid — she said so, and it was plainly true. "Now. Your blade, or my own blood. The ruin finishes either way. Choose. I'm not afraid. I checked that too."

The Ember said nothing. It had learned, at the pool, what Elin did with an ending she'd been handed.

Elin sheathed the blade.

She crossed the ruined grove and sat down beside the woman who had cured herself of a curse and been worn by a worse one, and she stayed, and she let Kaethra end as Kaethra — by her own failing blood, in her own steady time, with a witness. Not a mercy delivered. A death *attended*.

"...Sheathed," Kaethra said, and something that was almost a laugh moved through her. "Ha. Both camps armed a stranger and sent you in, and you end it with an empty hand." Her breathing was going. "That's the answer I'd have wanted carried back, bearer. Not *cure*. Not *acceptance*. *Witness.*" Her eyes found Elin's and held. "Tell Kesh the last thing the Root ever heard in here was me. Laughing at it. In my own voice."

She died that way, laughing, in her own voice, and the grove went quiet, and the white roots under it began — slowly, for the first time — to loosen their hold on the peat, because the thing that had wanted her had nothing left to want.

Elin sat with the body a while. She had signed a ledger in a frozen gallery — *someone must, I can* — and had not fully understood, then, what she was signing up to be. She understood now. Not the one who fixes. The one who *stays*. The one who does not look away from the ending, and does not let it happen alone, and carries it out whole to the people who need it carried. It was the smallest possible thing to be, and it was, she was beginning to suspect, the only thing worth being in a country full of gods who refused to let anything end.

---

Word ran ahead of her back through the Deep: it was done. At the cure-camp the drums beat the mourning rhythm, and at the pilgrims' rest the Choir sang something old and unsure — a liturgy with the certainty gone out of it, which is almost a prayer.

Kesh met her at the camp's edge, and Elin gave her the answer whole. *Witness.* And the last thing the Root heard was laughing, in her own voice.

"Both camps will carry her home together," Kesh said, when she could speak. "First thing we've done together in a generation, and it took *this*. *Cure or acceptance.* We built a whole people on that question, bearer. And she's the answer: it was a false choice, and she paid full price for asking honestly." She wiped her face, unashamed. "You gave her back her own voice at the end. Both camps will carry that further than either of us can throw a spear. Thank you."

The Blooming stalled behind Elin, with no one left to want it. And north, over the Thunder Plains, the horizon flickered — not lightning. The sky itself, tearing at the edges, void showing through the storm.

The last seal was straining. And Elin, who had refused the cure and kept her voice and learned at last exactly what her promise had always meant, turned toward the tearing sky and went to keep watch over the ending of the world she'd grown up in — carrying, in her chest, a fragment of a dead god that she had spared, and that had, in the sparing, become for the first time not her master and not her disease, but the thing she had decided, wholly and by herself, to keep.
