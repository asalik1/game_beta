# Appendix — The World of Vaelscar

*A reader's primer. It gives away no endings, but it names the pieces on the board. Read it whenever you like.*

---

## The Concord of Ash

Six hundred years before this story, the continent of **Vaelscar** tore itself apart in the **War of Cinders** — five god-kings, five armies, one long fire. It did not end in a victory. It ended in a **binding**: a working of the Concord of Ash that bound each god-king into the body of a mortal vessel, where the god would grow old and small and go to sleep.

The peace was built on a lie of omission. The gods were never gone. They were only quiet, sealed inside people, and each seal has been kept a different way — by ice, by verdict, by a lock frozen alive, by a sentence recited aloud without a single missed shift for six centuries. The names of four of the five god-kings were never spoken again; six hundred years of peace meant six hundred years of not saying them. They are known only by their epithets:

- **Mórwyn, the Hollow Flame** — blight, decay, rot-as-honesty. Her seal broke first, which is the only reason her name survives. Her faithful are the **Hollow Choir**.
- **The Molten Judge** — fire, the forge, verdicts. Always guilty.
- **The Still Queen** — ice, sleep, preservation. She does not kill. She *keeps*.
- **The Pale Root** — growth without death. The blight's green twin; it never subtracts, only adds.
- **The Storm Tongue** — storm, void, the unkept word. The sentence, mid-syllable, that has been waiting six hundred years to finish.

## The Ember Crown

Before the god-kings, the Concord's warding was carried by the **Ember Guard** — an order whose four founders each bore a primal fragment of fire, an **Ember**. Generations later a tyrant named **Vargoth** forced those four Embers to burn together as a single **Crown**, and wore the chain of them as a jewel, and made himself hollow and deathless upon it.

When the Crown was broken, it did not die. Chains shatter; fire only *scatters*. The four Embers flung themselves out into the world and went looking — rooting, one at a time, in ordinary people of the old Guard bloodlines. A person so chosen becomes a **shard-bearer**. They do not pick their gift. They discover which Ember caught in them, and inherit its **virtue**, its **temptation**, and the sins of the dead founder who carried it first.

Every bearer's power is a fragment of a tyrant's will. That is the thing they all have to decide what to do with.

## The Ember, up close

An Ember is not a tool. It is closer to a lodger with opinions. It leans toward what it wants; it goes quiet when a thing is *paid* for rather than taken; it purrs when it is fed. Each lineage tempts its bearer along one specific axis:

- The **warrior's** Ember (Vargoth's own) tempts toward *tyranny* — control them for their own good — and offers *protection*.
- The **mage's** Ember (Mórwyn's own — a battle-healer whose perfectionism curdled into blight) tempts toward *cruelty* — perfect them whatever it costs — and offers *clarity*.
- The **archer's** (Fangmaw's) tempts toward *severance* — owe no one — and offers *freedom*.
- The **assassin's** (a founder whose name the Guard erased) tempts toward *consumption* — take what keeps you strong — and offers *sacrifice*.
- The **paladin's** (a fragment of the Concord's own binding magic) tempts toward *righteousness* — I know what you need saving from — and offers *defiance*.
- The **warlock's** (a pact with something outside the world's edge) tempts toward the *debt spiral* — borrow more to fix the last borrowing — and offers *accountability*.

**Resonance** is the private ledger of how a bearer relates to the fire they carry — not good and evil, but *temptation and virtue*. The world feels it before any of them could name it: a flame leans toward a steady one, a sentry stands further off from a tempted one.

## The people who keep watch

- **Ser Aldric** — the man who killed Vargoth and shattered the Crown, and spent his own Ember doing it. Decades on, a burned-out legend who cannot fight and knows exactly what is coming.
- **Elder Maren** — trained under Aldric; now the last of the old keepers. She finds the newly-woken bearers before the factions do, and reads each one differently.

## The four factions

Two you can join; two you can only be watched by.

- **The Ember Accord** (Maren's people) — gather every shard, break the hollow throne, let no one wear the Crown again. They ask the strong to become *less* so the world can be *more*. It is not a wrong thing to ask. It is a very hard one.
- **The Cinderborn Compact** — old-regime nobles who prospered under the tyrant and miss not the man but the *roads*, the granaries, the law. Order requires a crown; find a worthy head for it. Not cartoon villains — people who lived well under a tyranny and told themselves it wasn't one.
- **The Wildfang Tribes** (ambient) — the descendants of Fangmaw's corrupted beastkin, fractured between a camp seeking a cure and a camp that has made peace with what it is and reads "curing" as conquest dressed as mercy.
- **The Hollow Choir** (ambient) — Mórwyn's blight-survivors turned faithful. The rot, they say, is the land's one honest truth. Some are grieving people who found meaning in horror. Some are genuinely dangerous. The line between is very hard to find, which is the whole point.

## Where this novel sits

**Act One** is the early game of a much longer war: L1–40, in the game's terms; the years in which the first seal strains, then cracks. The god-kings themselves stay off-stage. What the bearers fight, chapter by chapter, are their **heralds and their casualties** — the people the Waking has broken or bent or simply *asked*, and could not be refused.

The crack, when it comes, is not a defeat. It is worse than that. It is the thing the whole peace was built to prevent, delivered at last by the hand of someone who did everything right.
