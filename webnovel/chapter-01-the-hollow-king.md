# Chapter One — The Hollow King

The kingdom of Emberfall had fallen quiet, and Aldric had learned to distrust quiet more than he distrusted screaming.

Screaming, at least, told you where a thing was.

He came down out of the eastern passes in the grey before dawn, and the country he found spread below him was the country the songs still called green. The songs were sixty years out of date. The Darkwood had gone the colour of a bruise that would not finish healing; the river that fed the valley ran a shade too dark, as if something upstream had been dyeing cloth in it, or worse; and over the whole of it, patient as tombstones, stood the towers of the keep where a dead man had woken up and decided he was king again.

The Crown had been stolen. Word of that had reached even the passes — reached the handful of Ember Guard who were left, which was to say reached Aldric, because there was no one else the word could go to. The order that had once kept the seals of the world was three graves and one living man, and the living man was tired in a way that had nothing to do with sleep.

*You could turn around*, said the small warm thing behind his ribs.

He ignored it. It had been saying that for three days, and it would keep saying it, because that was what it was for. The Ember in him was not a weapon and was barely an ally. It was a candle someone had lit in his chest against a bonfire, and it knew the arithmetic as well as he did, and like anything small and alive it wanted very badly to keep burning.

"We're not turning around," he told it, out loud, because there was no one on the road to hear him be mad.

The Ember settled. It always settled. That was the frightening part — how reasonable it was, how quickly it made peace with whatever you'd decided, the way a thing makes peace with you when it has all the time in the world and you do not.

---

Emberfall Village still stood, which surprised him, and it still had people in it, which surprised him more.

They came out to look at him the way you look at weather — as a thing that might be good news and might flatten your roof, and either way you had no vote. A miller with a bandaged arm. A woman with a child's hand gripped too tightly in her own. An old man who had been a soldier once and stood, from long habit, at something like attention as Aldric passed, and then looked embarrassed about it.

The woman who mattered was waiting at the well.

She was young — younger than the work should have let her be — with the particular steadiness of someone who has decided that if the world is going to end it will at least be organized about it. She had a ledger under one arm and ink on two fingers and she looked at Aldric the way a good quartermaster looks at a shipment: what came, what's missing, what it's going to cost to make up the difference.

"Ser Aldric," she said. "Maren. I keep this place breathing. You're later than the birds said you'd be."

"The passes argue," he said.

"They will do that." She fell in beside him without being asked, matching his pace, which he approved of. People who waited to be invited to walk with you wasted a great deal of everyone's time. "You've seen the towers, then."

"I've seen them."

"Then you know the shape of it, and I'll only tell you the parts the towers can't." She said it low, so the child at the well wouldn't carry it home. "It isn't only him up there. When the Crown came loose, the rot came with it — the seals are old and the Crown was holding more of them shut than anyone wanted to admit. The Darkwood's wolves run with a dead master's warband still. The marsh has a witch in it who used to be a healer, and she is worse now than she was when she was only losing her mind. And him, at the top of it, wearing four fires like a hat and calling himself alive."

"Four fires," Aldric said.

"The Crown was never one thing." She glanced at him sidelong, and there it was — the thing he'd been half-hoping and half-dreading to find in her: she *understood*, at least a little, and understanding was a burden he'd have preferred to carry alone. "Four Embers. Four of the old founders' own. He chained them and put them on. If you unchain them—" She stopped.

"Say it, Maren."

"I don't know what happens if you unchain them," she said. "Nobody does. That's the honest answer, and I'd rather give you a true 'I don't know' than a comforting lie. Mórwyn's people used to trade in comforting lies. I watched what it did to them." She looked at his chest, at the thing she couldn't see but plainly knew was there. "You mean to spend it, don't you. Yours."

"You don't swing an ember at a god-crowned king and keep the ember," Aldric said. "I knew that walking out of the passes."

"Then walk it knowing this too," she said, and gripped his forearm once, hard, the way you grip a thing you're not sure you'll get to grip again. "Whatever's left of you after — there's a fire here, and a stump by it, and I don't let the good stumps go to just anyone. Come back and sit in it. That's an order from the woman who keeps this place breathing."

"That's not how orders work."

"It's how mine do." She let go. "The wolves first. Something they call Fangmaw leads the pack, and the road east isn't a road until it's dead. Take the potions. Keep moving. A still knight is a dead knight — I read that in a book you probably wrote."

"I didn't write books."

"Then someone wrote it about you," Maren said, "and either way it's true, so go."

---

The Darkwood took him in the way old woods take you in — all at once, and then completely, until the village behind might have been a place he'd dreamed.

He found the first shrines of the land's long grief before he found the wolves. A grandfather oak split open and hollow, and inside it wax stubs and a child's carved wooden wolf — offerings hidden for the wood's old spirits, from a time before the blight had given the wood new ones. The wax was recent. Someone still came here. Someone still believed there was an old kindness in the dark worth feeding, sixty years into being wrong about it.

He found a ravine that had nothing for him — no monster, no treasure, no door — but a view: the whole Darkwood rolling east under the mist, and the keep beyond it. He let himself have one long minute of it. He had learned, over a career of this, to take the minutes when the wood offered them, because the wood did not offer many and never twice.

He found a chapel of the Flame, sunk to its windows in black water, with a single candle burning on the altar stone above the waterline. The wax ran down it in years, not hours. The blight circled the drowned chapel and did not come in — could not, or would not; he could not tell which, and the distinction kept him standing there longer than was wise, wondering who had kept a candle lit for sixty years against a rot that could not cross the water, and whether they were still alive to keep it, and whether it mattered. The wind came off the black water and bent the flame, and it recovered, and bent, and recovered. Whoever minded this door was not here. For a moment the watch was anyone's.

He cupped his hand around the flame until the gust passed.

It was a small thing. It changed nothing. But for the length of one held breath the drowned chapel was exactly what it had been built to be — a lit room in the dark, with someone minding the door — and out on the water the blight's slow circling faltered, half a step, no more.

*Sentiment*, the Ember observed, not unkindly.

"Yes," Aldric agreed, and moved on, and the flame stood a little taller in the still air behind him, or he told himself it did.

The wolves found him at the Hollow, where the wood grew close and the light went the colour of old brass.

They were bigger than wolves. They were leaner than wolves too, which should have been a contradiction and wasn't; they had the look of things kept hungry on purpose, and they moved together the way a single mind moves a hand. Wound one, and its whole family answered. He had been warned. He had not needed the warning; he had fought Fangmaw's kind before the beast had a name, when they were only Guard hounds and their master was only a man.

Fangmaw came last, as the great ones always did. He crashed down out of the deep wood — a wolf the size of a cart, a wolf that wore its old master's cruelty like a second pelt — and where he landed the earth remembered it.

He had been the Ember Guard's beastmaster, once. Commander of the Stormwarden line. A man who could gentle a war-hound with a hand and a word, back when there had been a Guard to serve and hounds worth gentling. The corruption had taken the man and left the appetite, and the appetite had grown teeth and a following.

Aldric fought him for a long time, and did not enjoy it, and did not let himself look away from it either. The beast crouched and leapt and came down where Aldric had been; Aldric was elsewhere. It froze and flared and bolted in a straight furious line, and Aldric stepped once to the side and let the line spend itself on nothing. Halfway through, when it was hurt and knew it, it threw back its ruined head and *called*, and two more of the pack came out of the trees to die for it — because that was the last thing the beastmaster had left in him, the ability to make lesser things spend themselves on his behalf.

"You were better than this," Aldric told him, at the end, when the great sides were heaving and the brass light had gone red. "I remember you being better than this. I'm sorry no one reached you sooner. I'm sorry it's me, and not someone who knew your name when it was still yours."

The beast did not understand him. That was a mercy, possibly the only one on offer. Aldric ended it cleanly, and the wolves that were left did not scatter and did not flee; they came down quiet as falling leaves, took up their master, and dragged him away into the dark, and not one of them looked at Aldric as they went.

He stood in the emptied Hollow with his heart going hard and the Ember warm and satisfied in his chest, and he thought: *that is one head off the thing*. And then he thought about how many heads it had, and made himself stop thinking, and went to find the next.

---

The marsh was worse, because the marsh had a mind.

The Darkwood had been appetite. The Blightmarsh was *intention* — you could feel it in the way the poison pooled in patterns, in the way the dead did not simply lie where they fell but arranged themselves, in the way the cultists among the reeds sang while they came for you. They were mostly farmers, those cultists. Farmers who had lost everything to the rot and been told, by someone patient and gentle, that the rot had chosen them out of *love*. You pitied them right up until they swung, and then you pitied them after, which was worse.

She held the bower at the heart of it — Morwen the Blightcaller.

He knew what she had been before he ever saw her, because Maren had told him and because the marsh itself told him, in the drowned chapel's cousin down here, in a flooded shrine where the poison would not enter. Morwen had been a battle-healer of the Guard. A woman who had spent her gift closing other people's wounds, and had been so *good* at it, so relentless, so unwilling to accept that a body had a limit past which it could not be mended — that when the rot came whispering that death was only a failure of technique, she had already half-believed it herself.

She had heard the Hollow Flame first. She had heard Mórwyn — the god-king in her seal, stirring — and she had done what her faith's oldest tradition asked: she had taken the goddess's name. Cultists took the name. It was how they said *I have made room*. Morwen. Mórwyn. A single vowel's difference, and the difference was the whole point, and Aldric had not understood, walking into her bower, how much would turn on that single vowel.

"You've killed my hound," she said. She was thin and tall and her eyes were the wrong green, and her voice was gentle, genuinely gentle, the practised gentleness of someone who had told a thousand people that a thing would not hurt and been lying only some of the time. "You've killed my poor hound, and now you've come to kill me, and you think that ends it. Oh, ser. You've come sixty years too late to end anything, and about a year too early to understand what you're starting."

"Then explain it to me," Aldric said. "I'll wait. I've come a long way to hear a thing that's true."

"You wouldn't believe it. Nobody ever believes the healer." She raised her staff, and the marsh raised itself with her — the poison blooming up in rings, the bolts fanning out on a cadence he had to learn on the instant, the whole fen leaning toward him like a congregation leaning at a scaffold. "But I'll give you this much, for the road: I am not the thing you're afraid of. I am the *door* it's polite enough to knock on. Kill me, and you've knocked the door down, and whatever's on the other side comes through wherever it likes."

He did not believe her. He should have. That was the thing he would carry longest — not that she'd lied, but that she'd told him the truth and he'd taken it for a witch's riddle.

The fight was long and it was ugly and it was, at the last, a healer's fight: every time he thought he had her, the marsh mended what he'd broken, and he understood that he was not fighting a woman but fighting her *refusal*, the same refusal that had ruined her, the deep insulted certainty that nothing had to end if you were only skilled enough to keep it going. He beat the refusal out of her the only way it could be beaten — patiently, and completely, until there was nothing left to mend.

She went down among her reeds, and she was not gentle anymore, and she was not afraid either. She was *relieved*, which was the last thing he'd expected, and she used the relief to say one more thing, looking not at him but past him, north and east, toward a smudge of smoke on the horizon that he had taken for a village going about its evening.

"There," she said. "You feel that? She had to go *somewhere*. I was ready for her. I made room for her for thirty years. And you've just—" A laugh, wet and small. "You've just turned her out of a house she knew, ser, and now she's got to go and wake up in a stranger. Some poor soul who never asked. Some blacksmith, maybe, hammering away, wondering why her chest's gone hot." The wrong-green light guttered out of her eyes and left them ordinary, and human, and done. "You wanted to kill a god today. All you did was make her homeless. Flame keep whoever she lands on. Flame keep you. You're going to need it more than I do now."

She died believing she'd had the last word, and she had. It only took Aldric a year to hear it.

Far off, in a forge-village whose name he did not know, a blacksmith set down her hammer and put a hand to her breastbone, where something old and enormous had just, without permission, moved in.

---

The keep climbed.

He had thought the worst of it was behind him — the beast, the witch — and he was wrong, but not in the way he expected. The keep was not worse to fight through. It was worse to *walk* through. The Hollow and the bower had been the enemy's country; the keep had been *his*, once, in a manner of speaking — his order's, his tradition's — and Vargoth had made a slaughterhouse of it and then a mausoleum, and the two together were somehow more obscene than any marsh.

There was a cellar full of refugees who had run *toward* the tyrant's keep because the country around it was worse, and who looked at Aldric with the specific dead hope of people who have already survived the thing that was supposed to comfort them.

There was a collapsed tower with the burn-shadow of a guardsman printed on the masonry — a man who had stood his post the night Vargoth first rose, sixty years gone, and been calling for help at the end. Under the rubble, a signal-horn, its mouthpiece worn bright from a lifetime of use, its last call unfinished. Aldric picked it up. He held it a while. Then he set it upright on the stone, mouthpiece to the horizon, the way a signalman stands his post, and left the silence to the dead man, because some calls are not yours to finish.

And there was the reliquary — the one room the looters had somehow missed, or been afraid of. The Ember Guard's own. On a dusty plinth, the padded stand where the Crown had rested before Vargoth took it: empty sixty years, and the emptiness had a shape, the exact shape of the thing that should have filled it. Around it, the small honest relics of four founders. A whetstone. A folded letter. A ring. A child's silver whistle. The Ember in his chest knew this room. It had been *born* here, in a manner of speaking. It leaned toward the empty stand the way a plant leans toward a window, and for one long moment Aldric let it, and felt precisely how the thing would feel — right, fitted, overdue — and understood, in that moment, everything about how Vargoth had happened. It was not that the tyrant had been evil. It was that the Crown had felt like *this*, and he had said yes.

Aldric knelt to the founders instead, and renewed their oath in his own words. They came out plain and unheroic, which was how he knew they were the true ones. The Ember steadied — not tamed, but *enlisted* — and somewhere in its long memory, he thought, four dead people noted the signature.

Then he climbed the last stair to the Hollow Throne, and Vargoth stood up to meet him.

---

The tyrant was beautiful, and that was the last insult of the whole affair.

Not a rotting horror. Not a monster you could kill and feel clean about. A tall man in the prime the Crown had frozen him in, crowned in four braided fires, moving with the ease of someone who has not been afraid in six decades because he has arranged, at enormous cost to everyone else, never again to need to be. When he spoke, his voice was kind. That was the thing about all of them — Morwen, the poor doomed cultists, the beast that had been a good man. The kindness was real. It was just that it had been hollowed out and something else had moved into it, the way the Flame had moved into a blacksmith an hour's ride and a year's grief to the north.

"You've come a very long way to be disappointed," Vargoth said. "I know your order. I knew it when it had chapters. You've spent your whole small fire getting here, haven't you — I can see it, it's guttering. You want to spend the rest on me. And you will, and it won't be enough, and then this room will have you in it too, standing in a corner, wondering why the colours went dim." He smiled, and it was the warmest thing in the keep. "Sit down. Be my captain. I'll give you back the colours. I give things *back*, that's what they never understand about a crown — it's not a taking, it's a keeping."

"You're a keeping," Aldric agreed. "That's exactly what you are. You keep and keep and never once let anything end, and you call it order, and the marsh downstairs learned it from you and called it healing, and the throne under you learned it from something older and called it a god. I'm not here to argue with a keeping. I'm here to be an *ending*."

He did not remember most of the fight afterward, and he was glad of that. He remembered the blades that fell from the sky, each one marking where he'd been a breath before it landed, so that he never once got to stand still. He remembered the ground breaking under a slam and the slow ring of light rolling out from it in every direction, and threading himself out through the gaps between. He remembered, near the end, the moment Vargoth understood he might actually lose, and flared red, and roared, and moved half again as fast — the same enrage the beast had shown, the same the marsh had shown, the terror of a thing that has arranged never to be afraid discovering that the arrangement is failing.

And he remembered the blow. He would remember the blow until the colours came back, which was to say forever, because they never did.

He put everything he had left into it — the whole candle, all at once, in a single strike aimed not at the man but at the *chain*, at the place where four fires had been forced to burn as one against the will of every one of them. He felt his own Ember go into the strike and not come back. He felt the cold rush in behind it, the pilot light going out, the specific hollow that would be the shape of the rest of his life.

And he felt the Crown *speak*.

Not to Vargoth. To *him*. In a voice that was four voices and none, ancient and amused and utterly unhurried, it said — and he had thirty years, afterward, to try to mishear it kindly, and never managed —

*WELL STRUCK.*

*NOW WE CHOOSE OUR OWN.*

The chain did not break. Chains break; this was worse. The four fires *let go* — of Vargoth, of the chain, of the whole long argument they'd been forced to lose — and scattered, willing and delighted, out through the walls of the keep and away, four sparks flung to the four corners of the country, going *looking*. Vargoth came apart around the place where they'd been, sixty years of borrowed keeping collapsing all at once into the man he should have been allowed to be, which was dead. The throne room filled with a silence so complete that Aldric could hear his own pulse and nothing else, and the Ember that had been in his chest for three years was not in his chest anymore, and would never be again.

He had won. He understood that he had won. He stood in the emptied throne room, in a keep gone quiet, a hollow man over a hollow king, and understood that he had won, and that winning had been the smallest and cheapest part of it, and that the expensive part was only now beginning — was, in fact, riding out ahead of him on four fires and one homeless god, into a country full of ordinary people who had not asked for any of it and would spend the next several decades finding out.

---

Maren found him three days later, still on the road, walking west because west was where the fire had been.

She took one look at him — at the grey in a face that had not had grey in it, at the way he moved, at the specific stillness in the chest where a warmth used to argue — and she did not say *did you win*, because she could see that he had, and she did not say *are you all right*, because she could see that he was not, and never would be, in that one particular way.

She said, "There's a stump by the fire. It's yours. I said it was an order."

"That's still not how orders work," Aldric said.

"Come and sit down anyway," said Maren, "and tell me what you did, so that when it starts coming back on us — and it will, it's already started, there's smoke to the north that shouldn't be there — I'll at least know the shape of the thing we're paying for."

He went, because there was nothing else to do, and because the colours were dim and the bread was just bread and it turned out that even a hollow man would rather be hollow by a fire than on a road.

He told her all of it. The wolves, the drowned candle, the horn he'd left unfinished. And Morwen — Morwen most of all, and the smudge of smoke, and the blacksmith he'd never see, hammering away with a god waking up under her breastbone because he had kicked in the door of the house it had lived in.

"You didn't know," Maren said, when he was done.

"No," he agreed. "That's the part that won't stop being true. I did everything right, and it led here, and it'll keep leading here for the rest of my life." He looked into the fire, which was warm, and gave him nothing. "The Crown's out there choosing its own now. Four fires, in four strangers, one at a time, however long it takes. Every one of them a splinter of him." He nodded north, toward the smoke, toward the throne he'd emptied and the one that hadn't been built yet. "They're going to wake up ordinary people and hand them a piece of a tyrant's will and ask them what they're going to do with it."

"Then someone had better find them," Maren said, quietly, "before the worse people do."

Aldric looked at her for a long moment — young, and steady, and terribly capable, with ink on her fingers and the whole weight of a broken world balanced on the flat of her competence — and he felt something that a hollow man wasn't supposed to be able to feel anymore, which was hope, small and unwelcome and burning like a coal next to the bad arm.

"Yes," he said. "Someone had better."

To the north, in a forge-village whose name he would learn too late to matter, a blacksmith's hammer had gone silent. It would be a long time before anyone understood why. And far to the west, in a country that thought its war was over, four fires fell out of the sky and began, patiently, to look for the people they would choose.
