# Chapter Two — The Waking

Decades passed, the way decades do — not all at once, but completely.

The kingdom did not heal. It was never going to heal; that had been Aldric's whole grief, spoken by a fire that gave him nothing, and he had been right. What the kingdom did instead was *wake*. The blight that Morwen's death had turned homeless found a new house in a blacksmith, and the blacksmith was long gone now, but the god she'd carried was not, and it had spent the intervening years learning the country the way water learns a cellar — patiently, from the bottom, through every crack it was offered. The seals that the Crown had helped hold shut had gone on straining. The four fires had gone on choosing.

Elin did not know any of this. Elin knew that the ferryman's boy was going to die if she didn't do something, and that she was the only one in Millrow with hands that could do anything, and that it was past midnight and raining and the boy's mother was looking at her the way the drowning look at driftwood.

"Please," said the mother — Hesta, her name was, and Elin had known her since they were both girls filling buckets at the same slow river. "Please. You've done it before. You did it for the Aldenson twins, you did it for my own mother's hip. Please, Elin."

The boy was burning. Marsh-fever, the deep kind, the kind that came up the river in bad summers and took the small ones first. His name was Perrin and he was seven and he was the sort of child who filled his pockets with river-stones and named each one, and Elin had helped bring him into the world with these same two hands, and she was not going to stand in a doorway and watch the river take him back.

"Hold his shoulders," she said. "And whatever the light does, don't let go."

She laid her hands on him. She had done this before — a hundred times, a small warm gift she'd had since girlhood and never questioned, the way you don't question a good singing voice or a straight aim. You put your hands on the hurt and you *wanted* it mended, hard enough, and something in you leaned out and mended it. It had always been that simple. It had always been warm and gold and gone in a breath.

The light came green.

She felt it happen and she could not stop it — felt the warm gold thing in her chest that had always answered her *lean* differently this time, lean out and down and *deep*, into a place she'd never reached before, and pull something up from that place that was not warmth. The fever broke. She felt it break, felt Perrin's small body sag and cool under her hands, heard Hesta gasp with relief — and then she felt the other thing spread out from where the green light had gone in, a bloom of grey across the boy's ribs, cold to the touch, cold the way nothing living is cold.

She took her hands back. The fever was gone. In its place, over Perrin's heart, was a mark the colour of a drowned thing, and it was very slightly, very slowly, growing.

"He's cool," Hesta breathed. "Thank the flame, he's cool, he's—" And then she saw it, and her voice changed. "But — what is *that*? Elin. What did you *do*?"

*Nothing*, said a voice that had never spoken before, warm and close and reasonable, from exactly where the gold thing used to be. *You did nothing. The sickness ran deeper than it looked. Tell her that. She'll believe you — look at her, she wants to believe you, it would be a kindness. You did everything that could be done.*

It was the easiest thing in the world. The lie was already assembled, sitting on her tongue, fitted so perfectly to the moment that saying it would feel like telling the truth. And that — the *perfection* of it, how ready it was, how much it wanted to be said — was the thing that frightened her enough to say the other.

"My spell did this," Elin said. Her own voice came out rough. "Hesta. Look at me. *My spell* did this — the fever's gone and I did that, and this mark is here and I did that too, and I don't — I don't know how. I've never — the light's never come green before." She made herself keep looking at the woman's face while she said it, because looking away would have been the first small step onto the easy road, and something told her the easy road was very long and only went one direction. "But I am going to find how. And I am going to undo it. That's a promise, and I'll say it again in daylight when you don't have to believe me, and it'll still be true."

Hesta stared at her for a long moment. Perrin slept, cool and grey and breathing.

"You could have lied," Hesta said, finally. "I'd have believed you. I *wanted* to believe you." She sat down slowly on the edge of the bed, one hand on her son's cooling forehead. "Find how, Elin. I'll hold the promise. And—" She glanced up, and there was something new and wary in it, something Elin would see on a great many faces in the years to come. "—get some sleep before you touch anyone else. You've gone a colour I don't like."

Elin walked home through the rain, and the voice in her chest walked with her, and it did not sulk about being refused. It was patient. It said, mildly, as she let herself in and stood dripping in the dark:

*You'll come round to it. Not the lie — you're too honest, I can see that, I'll not waste us both by arguing. But the other thing. The mark. You said you'd undo it.* A warmth, almost fond. *You will need me for that. Undoing is only healing pointed backward, and I am very, very good at healing. Ask the boy. Ask his fever. We can fix him properly, you and I. We can fix* everyone*, if you'll only stop being squeamish about the method. Rest now. We've a great deal of good to do.*

Elin did not sleep. By morning the whole of Millrow's mill row knew that a spellwright had woken up wrong, and by noon there was a woman on a grey horse at her door who introduced herself, without ceremony, as someone who had been looking for Elin for a very long time.

---

The camp sat where the refugee roads crossed, in a hollow behind a palisade, close enough to the Waking's edge to matter and far enough to keep a fire lit. It had the look of a place that had been temporary for years — tents gone permanent, a cook-fire worn into the ground, a garden started in a spot that plainly used to be something else. People came here going somewhere, and some of them stopped, and the ones who stopped were the ones the roads had used up.

"Maren's camp," said the woman on the grey horse, who had given her name as Maren and had not slowed once in three days of riding to explain anything Elin actually wanted explained. She was old now — old the way a good tool is old, worn to exactly the shape of its work — and she had ink on two fingers still, which Elin found obscurely comforting, though she couldn't have said why. "Sit. The fire doesn't bite, which is more than I can say for most of what's left out there."

Elin sat. Around the camp, the world arranged itself into a set of small tableaux she would spend the rest of her life learning to read.

A sentry on the palisade, young and jumpy, who watched her the way you watch a dog you haven't decided about, and stood, she noticed, half a step further off than he stood from anyone else. *He can feel it*, the voice observed, interested. *They all can, a little. You're louder than you think.*

A widow by the cook-fire — Sera, a millwife who'd lost her mill on a river called the Greyrun when the water came up black one morning — who told Elin, unprompted and to no one in particular, that Maren believed the land could be cleaned, and that Sera suspected Maren believed it because someone had to.

A beastkin in a cage. Wiry, three generations off some corrupted Guard hound, watching everything with too-clever eyes and saying nothing, while the sentries argued in low voices about what to do with it.

A grey-wrapped pilgrim by the gate, swaying, humming a hymn with no words Elin knew, murmuring *the rot is honest, it only takes what was already leaving* to no one at all — and the sentries would not touch her, and Elin understood, looking at the grey habit, that this was the same grey the Hollow Choir's dead had worn, and that grief in this country had somewhere to go now, and where it went was not comforting.

And two people who wanted her. She could feel them wanting her before either spoke — a warden in the Accord's colours, hand resting with studied casualness near her hilt; a Cinderborn envoy in better tailoring than anyone else in the camp, smile already assembled. They were watching her the way the caged beastkin watched the latch.

"So," said Maren, ignoring all of it, reading Elin's face like the ledger she still carried. "Another one the shards chose. And this one, they tell me, chose *back* — told the ferryman's wife the truth to her face, with the mark still spreading." She nodded, slowly. "Mórwyn never once said *I don't know how*, spellwright. Not to anyone, not in sixty years. Hold on to those four words. They may be the most valuable thing you own, and you'll be offered a great deal to put them down."

"You know what's in me," Elin said. It was not a question. "You know whose it is."

"I know the shape of it." Maren's gaze was steady and not unkind. "Years since Vargoth fell, and the world didn't heal — it *woke*. The Crown's four fires rooted in people like you, one at a time, ordinary people, a piece of a dead king in every chest. The blight never stopped crawling. And now every power left in Vaelscar wants a shard-bearer on a leash." She leaned back. "I find the newly-woken before the factions do. What I ask is small — eyes, and honesty. What *they'll* ask is everything. You'll meet them today; I can't stop that and wouldn't. But before you decide anything, there's a man by that far fire you should talk to. He won't tell you what to do. He's the only one in this camp who's earned the right not to."

"Who is he?"

"Ask him yourself," Maren said. "And spellwright — when you're done, if you can still stand, come back and let me point you east. The Waking will not scout itself, and the road's the only teacher that's never once lied to me."

---

The man by the far fire did not look like a legend. That was the first thing.

He looked like an old soldier whose arm didn't work — she could see it in the way he sat, the way he'd arranged everything he might need on the good side. There was a stump by his fire, worn smooth, that he plainly kept for company. He watched her come with an expression she couldn't place until she was almost on him, and then she had it: he was looking at her the way you look at weather. As a thing that might be good news, and might flatten your roof, and either way you had no vote.

"Don't salute," he said. "The arm doesn't come up past the shoulder anymore, and returning it embarrasses us both. Sit, bearer. The fire's the only thing here that still burns properly." He tipped his head, studying her, and something moved behind his eyes. "Come closer where I can see you. Hm." A pause. "Yours is loud, isn't it. I can almost hear it from here." He said it without alarm, the way a doctor names a symptom. "Mine was loud too, near the end. Sit down anyway. It hates patience — that's the one thing it can't stand, so give it plenty."

Elin sat on the smooth stump. Up close he was older than old, and the fire in front of him gave off heat she could feel and, she understood suddenly, that he could not — not really, not the way she felt it. There was a stillness in his chest where a person like her expected an argument.

"You're Aldric," she said. "The one who killed the king."

"I'm the one who killed the king," he agreed. "Whether that's the same as the man in the songs, I've stopped being able to tell. Ask, then. Old men and dead kings both like being asked."

So she asked the thing she'd carried three days from Millrow, because he was the only person she'd met who might have an answer.

"What did it cost? Yours. When you spent it."

Aldric was quiet for a moment, feeding a stick to the fire with his good hand.

"Everything I was carrying," he said. "You don't swing an ember at a god-king and keep the ember. I knew that walking in — that part they get right in the songs." He watched the flame. "What they don't tell you is the *after*. The colours go dim. Bread is just bread. The fire in me went out and it took the pilot light with it — the little warmth under everything, the one you don't know is there until it isn't." He looked at her. "And still. Cheapest thing I ever bought, that blow. Remember that, bearer, when yours starts telling you what you can't afford to lose. It's a liar about prices. They all are. They quote you the cost of *losing* the fire and never once the cost of *keeping* it."

*He's bitter*, the voice murmured. *Look at him. He gave everything and he's got a stump and a fire he can't feel. Is that the sermon? Is that who you'd like to be?*

Elin ignored it, which was getting easier, and asked the next thing.

"Maren gave me the recruiting version of what the Crown is. Four fires, a dead king, a piece of him in every chest." She watched his face. "What's the real version?"

"Ah." Something almost like pleasure crossed the old face — the pleasure of a man who's been waiting a long time for the right question. "The armory version. Good. Here it is, then, and you'll get it from no one else because no one else was in the room." He set the stick down. "It was never *one* thing. Four embers, carried by the Guard's four founders, forced to burn as a single crown. Vargoth didn't steal a symbol. He chained four old fires together, against every one of their wills, and wore the chain as jewelry." He held up his good hand, fingers spread, then closed it slowly to a fist. "So when people say the Crown *shattered* — no. Chains shatter. Fires *scatter*. That thing in your chest is one of the original four, or a splinter off one, and it remembers being free. Every shard-bearer in Vaelscar is carrying a piece of an argument that started six hundred years ago, and every one of you thinks the argument is new, and about you." He almost smiled. "It isn't. That's the first mercy and the first insult, and it's the same fact."

Elin sat with that. The fire popped. Across the camp, the Choir pilgrim's wordless hymn rose and fell.

"There's more," she said. "You've got the look of a man holding something back."

The old knight regarded her for a long moment. Then he glanced, once, at the sentry on the palisade — checking the distance — and lowered his voice, and Elin understood that she was about to be given a thing that had not been given before.

"You've the mark of the east road on you already," he said. "You'll walk it, and you'll see what the Waking does, and then you'll understand why I never said this to Maren, who'd have hunted every last one of you down for caution's sake if I had. So hear it now, and carry it, and decide for yourself what it means — that's the whole of my hope, and I keep it right here next to the bad arm." He leaned in. "When my blade went in — when the chain came apart — the Crown *spoke*. Not to Vargoth. To *me*." His voice did not shake, which was somehow worse than if it had. "I've had thirty years to mishear this kindly, bearer, so believe me when I tell you I haven't. It said: *Well struck. Now we choose our own.*"

The fire seemed very loud.

"It wasn't defeated," Aldric said. "It *dismissed* itself. The scattering wasn't an accident of my blow — the fires went *looking*. Which means every one of you was *picked*, deliberately, by something with six hundred years of patience and a grudge against chains." He watched her take it, watched it land, and — she would think about this for years — he did not look away while it did. "Your ember didn't fall into you, spellwright. It *chose* you. Out of everyone. For a reason. And here's the only sword I have left to give anyone, so take it: being chosen is not the same as being owned. The thing picked you. But *you* pick what the reason means. It cannot make you into anything. It can only offer, and offer, and wait — and hope you're too tired, one day, to keep saying no."

Elin became aware that the voice in her chest had gone completely silent. Not sulking. *Listening* — the specific, attentive quiet of a thing that has just heard itself described accurately by someone who got away.

"It's very quiet now," she said. "In here."

"Yes," Aldric said, leaning back, lighter by exactly one secret. "It would be. It doesn't like being explained. Go on, bearer. Mind the east road." He turned back to his fire, which gave him nothing, and which he tended anyway. "And mind the voice more."

---

The factions caught her before she reached Maren, because that was their whole art.

The Accord warden got to her first — Callis, a hard woman with buried friends behind her eyes. She did not circle it. "The Accord wants every shard gathered and the hollow throne broken for good," she said. "Yours included, one day, with your consent. We ask people to become *less* so the world can be *more*. It's a terrible pitch. It's also the only one where the strong end up voluntarily smaller instead of somebody else's god." She looked Elin over, and — remarkably — relaxed a degree. "You hold it well. That's exactly who we want. Few would carry the asking better than you."

And the Cinderborn envoy, right behind, smile sharpening — Vessa, in her good tailoring. "Before Maren's people fill your ears," she said, "understand: we do not miss the tyrant. We miss *roads*. Granaries. Law. A crown is a tool, spellwright, and Vaelscar is bleeding for the lack of one. Work with us and be paid, protected, and *remembered*. History belongs to whoever holds the paperwork."

Elin told them both, honestly, that crowns and she were having a complicated moment, and that she'd keep her own counsel a while longer. Callis said the offer kept, that the Accord was patient the way stone was patient. Vessa said complicated moments passed but poverty and banditry historically did not, and that Elin would know the Cinderborn colours when she tired of camping. Neither of them was wrong, which Elin was beginning to understand was the whole trouble with everyone.

She stopped, on her way to Maren, at the cage.

The beastkin pressed itself to the far bars as she came — the first fear it had shown anyone, she'd hear later. "Two hungry things," it rasped, "and only one of us is caged." Then, when she only looked at it: "Shard-carrier. Your pack or mine, a cage is a cage. The Tribes remember who opens doors. And who watches."

The voice in her chest had an opinion, of course. *Leave it. It raids grain carts. It's Fangmaw's blood — it's the same argument you're carrying, wearing fur. Nothing you owe.*

Elin unlatched the cage.

"Run," she said, "before the sentries agree on anything."

It was over the palisade before the latch stopped swinging, and from the treeline came one short howl — a note, not a threat. Something out there was keeping accounts, and had just, she understood, entered her onto the generous page. The voice said nothing about it, which she was learning to read as displeasure, and which she was learning, further, to enjoy.

---

Maren pointed her east, as promised, and east was where the Waking showed its work.

The road ran down out of the camp's high country and into land the blight had been at for years — not the acute horror of a fresh wound, but the settled wrongness of a chronic one. Trees that leaned the wrong way. A silence with a texture to it. And, threading through all of it, the Greyrun — the river Widow Sera had lost her mill on — running black as ink under a sky that couldn't account for it.

Elin found the mill.

It stood. That was the thing that stopped her on the bridge — it *stood*, sagging and empty and reclaimed at the edges by the black water, but standing, and on the front of it a door still painted a stubborn, defiant blue. Twenty years of spring paint, Sera had said, to no one, back at the camp. Elin had not meant to come this way for a widow's door. But the door held, and the paint was, against every odd in this country, winning, and Elin found she wanted very much for someone to know it.

*Sentiment*, said the voice, and this time it did not sound fond. It sounded like a thing filing away a weakness. Elin thought of Aldric cupping a flame no one had asked him to keep, and did not argue, and made a note to carry the widow her news whole.

The Waking's makers found her a mile past the mill.

They had been people. That was the first hard lesson of the east road and it would be the lesson of every road after: the things she had to put down had names once, and neighbours, and reasons. Blight-mad and grief-mad and simply *emptied*, they came at her out of the black reeds, and she learned that day what her hands were for now — that the same lean that had mended Perrin could be pointed *outward*, could be made to do harm as easily as it had once done good, more easily, because harm asked less precision. She did not enjoy it. She made herself not enjoy it, which the voice found tiresome and said so.

And on the ridge above the black river she met the first of the Waking's broken great ones — a thing that had been a man, a commander, storm-touched and long past reason, wearing lightning like Fangmaw had worn cruelty. Korrag, the survivors downriver called him. Stormwarden, of an order she'd never heard of, of the same old Guard line that had bred the beast Aldric killed. The Waking, Maren had told her, was pulling the old bloodlines apart first — and here was the proof of it, a keeper unkept, a storm that had broken its own master.

He was not the reason the storm was breaking. She didn't understand that yet, and wouldn't until she stood, an act of grief later, on a summit at the edge of a tearing sky. She only understood that he was in agony, and dangerous, and that his lightning came in lashing lines she had to read on the instant, and that when she'd finally grounded the last of it and the storm went out of him, what was left was a tired old face that looked, for one moment before it stopped, almost relieved.

*You could have kept some of that*, the voice said, quietly, as the storm's residue crackled and died in the wet grass. *All that power, going to waste in the mud. You're going to need it, out here. He wasn't using it well. You would.*

"No," Elin said, to the empty ridge, to the black river, to the thing in her chest that was Mórwyn's own and would spend the rest of her life offering her exactly what she most wanted and had just enough sense to refuse. "That's how it starts. That's how it started with *her*. I'm not keeping the storm."

The voice went quiet. Listening again. It had a great deal of patience, and she had a great many miles left to go, and it was content, for now, to walk them with her and wait.

She carried Widow Sera her news on the way back — *it stands, the paint is winning* — and watched a woman who had stopped saying *we had a mill* sit slowly down on a cook-bench and start, tentatively, to believe that some of the land was clean already. It weighed nothing, that small honest cargo. It was worth the whole trip.

Then she turned east again, because the Waking was spreading faster than she could scout it, and because Maren had had another letter, and because east of the camps the Choir's heartland had gone quiet in the wrong way — a funeral, they said, that had been going on for sixty years, and had just noticed someone coming to end it.
