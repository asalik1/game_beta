# Chapter Four — The Slagfields

The land south of the Vale burned upward instead of down.

Elin had thought she understood fire — she was a mage, in the way the world used the word; she could put heat into her hands and out again. But the Slagfields were fire as *institution*. Whole hillsides of ash flats gave way to foundries built over the vents, and the foundries ran day and night, and the heat that fed them came up out of the deep rock at temperatures no coal could explain, and everyone she met had the same look: the look of people who had told themselves a very convenient thing for a very long time and had recently stopped being able to.

The man at the Cinder Gate was named Brann, and he was an overseer of what was left of the southern foundry line, and the first thing he said to her was a ledger.

"You're the one who opened the Vale road," he said. "Good — freight's moving, which means my problems are now arriving *faster*." He didn't smile. He had the face of a company man whose numbers had stopped balancing four crews ago, and he'd heard already how she'd done what she'd done up north, and he studied her with a wary respect. "I've got people down there I'd want handled the same way, if it comes to it. The gentle way. Sit. Let me give you the honest ledger, since honesty's cheaper than everything else down here lately."

The honest ledger was this. The Cinderborn Compact had reopened the foundries two years ago — the best ore vein in Vaelscar — and told themselves the strange high heat was a *gift*. Then the gift started answering. Brann's finest smith, a woman named Calda, had a run of blades that stopped breaking. A beast came up out of the deep vents and ate four crews. And the chaplain — Ordo, whose requisition Brann had signed himself, flame help him, when the man asked to hold his services closer to the heat — had started ending his sermons with *verdicts*.

"Three problems, in order of depth," Brann said. "Calda at the quenching yard. The beast in the vents. And Ordo at the bottom, under everything." He looked at her. "Your people up north got in over something old, is my guess. Something under this rock. I'd call it a *well* and not a windfall — which makes me the first man through that gate to talk about it that way, and I don't love what that says about the rest of us."

*He's right about the well*, the Ember said, warm and interested. It had been interested since the gate. *There's a court under here. A real one. Can't you feel it weighing? I could teach you what it knows. You've been carrying a question a long time now — the boy, the mark, the "I don't know how." The thing under this mountain doesn't say "I don't know how." It has never once said it. Wouldn't that be a relief?*

"Calda first," Elin said, to Brann, and to the thing in her chest. "I'll cap the well, not admire it."

She met Petra on her way out of the gate — a smith of the fifth crew, the crew whose memorial plaque hung blank downstairs because Petra kept, stubbornly, not dying. "Everyone else went in the vents," Petra said. "I was topside with a broken wrist. Clumsiest day of my life, and the only reason I have a rest of my life." She looked at Elin the way people were starting to look at Elin — measuring the heat in her, the lean of the thing she carried. "Give me a reason to sharpen my good chisel."

"Give me their names," Elin said. "When the beast is dead, they go on the plaque. All nine, carved proper. Not 'crew five, with regret.'"

Petra told her the names. Aldan. Merit. Bosk. Hale. The twins. Ruta, Sef, and a boy they'd all just called Ember, because he was new, flame, he was so new. "Nine names," Petra said, and her voice did something. "You asked. Two years, and the Compact never once asked."

---

Forgemistress Calda was the best smith in the Slagfields, and the horror of her was that she still was.

"Stand in the light, bearer," she said, when Elin found her at the quenching yard. "Let me see the temper of you." She was long and sharp and moved like her own blades, and there was a fever-brightness in her that had nothing to do with the heat. "Mm. Forged fast, quenched early. Whoever made you was *rushing*." She smiled. "The Judge whispers tolerances no mortal forge can hold, and my blades hold them anyway. Come. Let's find out what *you* hold."

That was the thing about the Slagfields, Elin was learning: nobody down here had been *conquered*. They had been *promoted*. Calda had asked the deep rock how to be perfect and the deep rock had told her, and now her mistakes didn't break any more than her successes did, and she was radiant with it, and she fought Elin with a weapon that heated over the course of the fight until it burned, and marched to the slag pools at the yard's edge to quench and cool and come back *stronger*, unless Elin stood in the pool herself and made her quench through a person instead.

Elin denied her the pools where she could and ate the hardest hits where she couldn't, and at the end Calda looked down at her own blade — at the crack running its length, the first of her edges ever to fail — and said, wonderingly, "...out of tolerance," the way a woman says a word in a language she'd forgotten she knew, and let it go, and let herself go with it.

Below the yard, down the vent shafts, something enormous shifted its weight against the rock, and the foundry bells rang the cave-in code for the fifth time that season.

---

She found the Ember Font before she found the beast, and the Font was the real fight of the Slagfields, though nothing swung at her in it.

The foundry maps marked the chamber DO NOT DIG. Inside was a font of living slag, perfectly round, bubbling at blood-heat, and the Ember in her chest leaned toward it the way iron leans toward a lodestone — because there was a voice in the slag, faint and measuring, weighing and finding wanting, and it was speaking her ember's native tongue.

*This is where Calda came*, the Ember said, and for once it was not coaxing; it was hushed, almost reverent. *Listen. Just listen. It will teach you* tolerances*. How hard to swing, exactly. Where the flaw in any guard lives, exactly. Where the flaw in any* body *lives — do you understand what I'm offering you? You could go back to Millrow and put your hands on that boy and know* exactly *what you did and* exactly *how to reverse it. No more "I don't know how." The Judge doesn't traffic in "I don't know how." Lean close. It's free.*

And Elin stood at the edge of the font, and she wanted it. That was the thing she would not tell Maren, or Ilse, or anyone. She *wanted* it — not the power, but the *certainty*, the end of the not-knowing, the perfect knowledge that would let her keep a promise she had no idea how to keep. Every part of her that had ever failed a patient leaned toward the slag with the thing in her chest.

Then she thought of Mórwyn. The battle-healer who could not accept that a body had a limit. Who had been so *good*, so relentless, so unwilling to say *I don't know how* — that when the rot came and told her death was only a failure of technique, she had already half-believed it. The Font was offering Elin the exact road Mórwyn had walked. It was offering to cure the *I don't know how*, and the I-don't-know-how was the only thing standing between Elin and being the thing in her chest.

She did not lean close and listen. Instead she plunged her blade-hand into the slag for one full breath — paid the pain honestly, took nothing, and let the Judge weigh *that*.

The pain was total, and clarifying, and the voice in the slag went *silent* — genuinely surprised. The slag did not burn what would not bargain with it; she took her hand back whole. Somewhere far below, she felt a verdict quietly vacated, a case set aside, and the Ember in her chest sat straighter for days, chastened, and — she thought — the smallest bit relieved, the way a thing is relieved to be refused by someone it can respect.

She still did not know how to save the boy. She walked out of the chamber carrying the not-knowing like a lamp.

---

Cinderhide, in the deep vents, was almost a rest.

It was a slag-beast a meter thick in cooled obsidian, and it had eaten four crews because steel didn't bite it — but the lava did. The plating that made it near-invincible *melted* in the arena's own fire, so Elin fought it the way you fight weather: not by matching it, but by reading the ground, baiting its Fangmaw-blooded charge across the pools, letting the mountain do to it what no blade could. Four memorial plaques bolted the wall — crews one through four — and a fifth hung blank, waiting.

When its plating finally cracked along the seams the lava had taught her, what poured out was heat with nothing left to burn, and Cinderhide settled into the vent it had been born from and cooled for the last time. She thought of Petra's chisel. She thought of nine names. The fifth plaque wouldn't hang blank much longer.

From up the concourse, carried on the vent draft, came a voice. Measured. Warm. Ending every sentence the same way. The sermon had been running for days.

---

The Foreman's Court found her before the sermon did, and it was the hardest thing in the Slagfields, and no one attacked her in it either.

Nine foundry workers ringed a kneeling man — Foreman Dask, who had sealed a vent door with crew five still inside. *Procedure*, he kept saying, to the floor. The crew hadn't touched him. They were *waiting*. And when they saw Elin — armed, shard-lit, arriving out of the heat like something *sent* — every face turned to her with the same terrible hope.

"You judge," one of them said. "Whatever you say, we'll do."

The Ember agreed, warmly, before anyone else could speak. *Yes. This is precisely what verdicts are for. Look at them — they're begging to be led. You've seen the case; you can feel the shape of it; the Judge under this mountain is offering you the words right now, they're on your tongue, all you have to do is open your mouth and say* guilty *and nine broken people will love you like a god. Isn't that what clarity is? Knowing? Deciding? You're so* good *at deciding.*

And it was true, the terrible part. She could feel the verdict assembling itself, fitted so perfectly to the grief in the room that saying it would feel like justice. Nine faces, flooding with relief before she'd even spoken. It would be *so easy*. It was, she realized — with the specific cold that came whenever the thing in her chest showed her its actual face — the exact same easiness as the lie in Hesta's doorway. The perfect, ready, seductive sentence that wanted so badly to be said.

"No," Elin said.

The word landed in the hot chamber like a dropped tool.

"He goes topside for a real trial," she said. "You are grieving, and I am armed, and neither of us gets to be a court. That's the whole reason there are courts, so that the grieving and the armed don't get to be one." She looked at the nine faces, at the relief curdling into something more complicated and more human. "I won't hand you a verdict. I'm sorry. It's the one thing I could give you that you'd regret."

For a long moment nothing moved. Nine grieving people, one kneeling foreman, and a whisper under the floor that had no appeal for the thing she'd just done. Then the oldest of the nine let out a breath that seemed sixty hours old. "...Topside," she said. "Aye." And they bound Dask's hands gently, like people handling the last fragile thing they owned, and led him up toward a trial that might convict him or might not, and either way would not be theirs, or hers, or the Judge's.

The whisper under the floor said nothing. Refusing an offered gavel, it turned out, was a verdict it could not overturn.

---

Ashpriest Ordo was waiting in the Verdict Hall, and Ordo was what the Slagfields were *for*.

"Ah," he said, when she came in. "The court was expecting you. You're on the docket, bearer. You have been since the Vale." He was a chaplain in Cinderborn grey, or had been, and he was warm and reasonable and completely certain, and the fires in the hall leaned toward him when he spoke. "I listened to the forge-deep for one night, and the Judge gave me the only sermon that never lies. *Guilty.* The land, guilty. The empire, guilty. You—" He spread his hands. "Well. We needn't spoil the verdict."

They fought under a hall that split down its middle — one half judged, then the other, full-arena eruptions with a single safe side, so that Elin spent the fight *reading the ruling* and getting to the innocent ground before it fell. He announced each one. *Guilty: the west.* And at intervals he called up his Sons of the Judge, slow ember-things that marched toward him to be consumed, each one that reached him making his verdicts come faster, so that she had to choose, every cycle, between stopping his sermon and stopping his reinforcements.

He was the herald of a god-king, and Elin, fighting him, understood the shape of the whole act at last — understood what the Accord's warden Edda had told her at the gate, that the seals did not break from the *outside*. They broke from *attention*. Every sermon Ordo preached, every whisper Calda had obeyed, every ear put to a font that said DO NOT DIG, was a hand on the lid of a sleeping god. The Slagfields were not being invaded. They were being *listened to* awake.

"You never acquit," she said to him, dodging a verdict, and it was not a taunt; it was the thing she'd learned from a lay preacher on the road, the thing that broke the Judge's spell if you said it slow enough. "Name one soul the fires found innocent. A court that can't acquit isn't a court. It's just a fire that learned to talk like one."

Ordo did not have an answer, because there wasn't one, and a herald with no answer is only a man in over his head. She ended his sermon mid-sentence.

The fires in the hall leaned toward his body like a congregation leaning over a casket — then straightened, and went ordinary, and were only fires again.

---

Brann met her at the mouth of the works, in the cooling dark.

"The Compact will say a mad chaplain died and the foundries are safe," he said. He looked shaken in a way company men don't let themselves look. "I counted what the fires did just now, bearer. When he fell. They *answered* him — leaned in, the whole hall, before they let go. The verdicts were never his." He passed a hand over his face. "Something under this rock has been finding my crews guilty for two years and signing it in Ordo's hand, and I signed the requisition that let it get close enough to try. I'll carry that. But you capped it. Whatever that thing is, it's back to a whisper, and my people can quench in water again if they've the sense to."

Somewhere in the works, Petra was already carving. Nine names, cut proper.

"North, then," Brann said, and nodded at the road that bent away from the heat into weather that never changed. "The stories coming down that road don't make sense — villages where nobody wakes up, and nobody dies, and wagons rolling north full of sleepers. I stopped believing it. Then I stopped being able to." He looked at her, this tired honest man over a cooling well of a god. "Go careful. And bearer — thank you for asking the nine. That's the whole of what the Compact never does, and it turns out to be the whole of the thing."

Elin went north, into the changeless weather, carrying a lamp made of *I don't know how* and a promise she still couldn't keep — but hadn't traded for false certainty, which she was beginning to understand might be the only victory the Slagfields had on offer. Behind her the fires burned ordinary. In her chest, the thing that was Mórwyn's own had gone thoughtful, and had stopped, for a while, offering her the end of her not-knowing, because it had watched her plunge her hand into a god's certainty and take nothing out, and it was recalculating what, exactly, it had chosen.
