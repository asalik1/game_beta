# Chapter Five — The Long Sleep

North of the foundries the weather stopped changing, and then it stopped doing anything at all.

The Frozen Expanse was not cold the way winter is cold. Winter is a process — it comes, it does its work, it goes. This cold had *arrived* and *stayed*, the way a guest stays when the guest is the house now. Whole villages sat under it, and Elin walked into the first of them expecting the dead and found, instead, the sleeping: families in their beds, frost-lace on their folded hands, breathing once a minute, cool and grey and *smiling* — every one of them wearing the same smile, the exact same smile, as if something had drawn it on.

The Ember in her chest did not like the smiles. That was new. In the Vale it had been at home; in the Slagfields, interested. Here it was *uneasy*, and Elin, who had learned to read the thing the way she read a fever, filed the unease away as important.

*She and I want the same thing*, it admitted, when she pressed it, standing over a sleeping child who would not wake and would not die. *That's what's wrong with this place. The Queen and me — the same wish, different roads. I refuse endings by saying nothing really rots, not all the way down. She refuses them by* stopping the clock. *It's the same lie wearing two coats. And I find I don't like meeting it in someone else's mouth. It sounds so much more* reasonable *than I do.*

"That's the first useful thing you've ever told me," Elin said.

*Enjoy it. I'm not being generous. I'm being* territorial. *There's only room in you for one thing that refuses endings, bearer, and it had better be me and not her.*

The woman waiting at the Last Fire — the last honest fire before the ice took over entirely — was a Wildfang tracker named Yri, of the winter clans, and she led with the truth, which Elin was learning to trust.

"Before anything else, understand what you're walking into," Yri said. "Nobody out there is evil. That's what makes this one hard." She fed the fire and did not look away from it. "The Long Sleep cult carries sleepers north — their own kin, freely given, to wait for the Queen's morning in the deep ice. Madness. But *gentle* madness. And here's the knot: they pay the winter clans in grain for safe passage. Famine snow, bearer. That grain is the only reason my cousins' children have marrow in their bones this year."

"And someone holds the road," Elin said.

"Hrolgar Whitepelt. Chieftain of the *we-are-what-we-are* clan, holds the Watch Ridge for the cult." Yri's jaw set. "He is not corrupted. Not mad. Not cruel. He is a man who found one way to feed forty mouths and stopped asking what the wagons carry. You'll have to move him, and he won't move, and I am asking you to *know* all of that before the ridge — not after, when knowing does nobody any good."

"I'll give him every chance to stand aside," Elin said. "And carry it plainly if he won't."

Yri nodded slowly. "Every chance. He won't take it. But the offering matters. It'll matter to the clans, after." She looked at Elin with the flat gaze of someone who counted things for a living. "And bearer — however it ends up there, the clans will be listening to *how you tell it*. Winters are long. We remember things in winter."

---

She met the cult before she met the ridge, and the cult was worse than any monster, because the cult was *right* about the one thing it needed to be right about.

Gentle Suli drove wagons for the Long Sleep, and she met Elin's eyes without flinching. "You've the look of someone about to do violence to my faith," she said, "so let's be civil first. My daughter is on one of those wagons. I put her there. She was dying, bearer. Now she is *dreaming*, and when the Queen's morning comes she wakes healed. That's the promise. Sixty fevers, forty famines, one promise. And here is why you'll fail to talk me out of it: nobody who sleeps has *died*. Not one, in nine years. Show me another faith in Vaelscar with that record."

Elin, who was a healer, felt the pull of it in her own two hands. *Nobody has died.* She had knelt at more deathbeds than she could count. She had a grey mark on a boy's heart back in Millrow that she still didn't know how to lift. And here was a faith that promised the one thing she had never once been able to promise anyone: *not yet. Not ever, if you'll only sleep.*

"Nobody's died because nobody's *finished*, Suli," she said, and it cost her to say it, because half of her wanted the promise as badly as the wagon-driver did. "A held breath isn't a record. Ask the thing at the keystone what the Queen keeps things *for*."

Suli's face closed, and then, unexpectedly, opened again — into something worse than an argument. "Kept things," she said. "*Kept*—" She stopped. "I'll tell you what I've never told the shepherd. Some nights, driving, I hear the ice under the wagons. And it doesn't sound like a nursery, bearer. It sounds like a *pantry*." She turned back to her traces. "Her name was Wren. Nine years old, forever now, I suppose. You're the first southerner to ask the name instead of the doctrine. The doctrine I can defend all night. The name I just miss. Go on. Do whatever you came to do to my faith. Gently, if there's a gentle way."

---

Hrolgar Whitepelt was waiting on the Watch Ridge, and he did not pretend.

"Stop there, bearer. I know why you've come, and I want you to hear it from me plain: I know what's in the wagons. I've known all winter." He was a huge man gone grey, and there was no madness in him and no cruelty, only the terrible flatness of a sum that has only one answer. "The cult feeds my clan. Forty mouths, famine snow. And the price is this ridge. You'd have done the same." His pack circled the rocks above, wolves gone strange around the sleeping valley, shepherds of the dreaming. "You're about to prove you'd do *worse*."

She fought him because there was no other door, and it was not a triumph and never felt like one. His wolves slid on the ice; his charge, on the ice, could not stop, and overshot, and slammed the arena wall, and she learned to bait it and punish the skid — arena-reading as the only skill on offer, the fight itself a kind of grim arithmetic to match the man's. When he fell, his pack did not scatter. They came down off the rocks, quiet as snowfall, took up their chieftain's body, and dragged him home through the drifts, and not one of them looked at her.

The ridge stood open. It did not feel like a victory, because it wasn't one. It was policy.

She went back to the Last Fire before she went on, because Yri had asked her to, and because a debt was a debt.

"Hrolgar Whitepelt is dead," Yri said, "and his pack dragged him home, and forty mouths are asking what happens to the grain. So tell me straight, bearer, because the clans will hear it in *your* words. What happened on my ridge?"

Elin thought about it — thought about the flat sad certainty in the big man's face, the forty mouths, the canvas he'd never lifted. She could call him a toll-keeper. It would even be true, and the Accord would thank her for the clean framing.

"A chieftain held the only line he had," she said instead, "and lost to a stronger hand. Your clans should sing him honest. I'll say so anywhere."

Yri was quiet for a moment. "...Sing him honest," she said. "Aye. The clans will hear that a stranger spoke of Hrolgar like a chieftain and not a toll-gate — and that buys more peace than the grain ever did. I'll see the grain replaced somehow." She looked at Elin, and something in the tracker's hard face eased. "Walk warm, bearer. You just did."

---

The wagon was on the road between the ridge and the keystone, stopped mid-journey, its porter frozen dead in the traces days ago and still leaning north.

Under the frosted canvas: eleven sleepers, laid like cordwood, breathing once a minute. One was a child clutching a wooden horse.

North lay the deep ice and the Queen's promised morning. South lay the waystation, and fires, and the slow lottery of waking cold — some of them would rise, and some would never rise, and there was no way to know which without trying. The wagon could not stay on the road. Where it went appeared to be Elin's to decide, and the Ember in her chest went very quiet, the way it went quiet at the real ones.

*Careful*, it said, and it was not tempting her; it was *warning* her, which frightened her more. *This is the Queen's whole argument, right here in a cart. Haul them north and every one of them is safe — safe forever, safe the way your boy's fever is safe, sleeping and grey and never once at risk of anything again. Nobody dies. You could give eleven families that. You've spent your whole life unable to give one family that.*

And there it was — the temptation of the whole frozen country, aimed with perfect knowledge at the one wound Elin carried. She was a healer. The Queen offered the healer's impossible dream: *no one you touch will ever die.* All it cost was that no one would ever wake, either. All it asked was that she trade the risk of an ending for the certainty of a stopped clock.

She thought of Varo, and the door unlocking from the inside, and how the only mercy the whole Vale had wanted was to be *allowed to end*. She thought of Suli, hearing a pantry under her wagons. She thought of the grey smile drawn on eleven faces, the same smile, exactly the same.

She hauled the wagon south.

The traces bit her shoulders for two frozen miles. At the waystation they took the sleepers in by the fire, and by dusk three were shivering — and shivering, she understood, is what waking cold feels like, and it was the best thing she had heard in weeks. By night one of them, the child, opened her eyes and demanded, furious, to know where her horse was.

It was in her hand. She was *holding* it.

Some of the others might never wake. Elin had chosen their risk without their leave, and she would carry that; it was not a clean thing she had done, only a true one. But the child was holding the horse, and was *angry*, and being angry is a thing only the living get to do, and Elin stood in the waystation's small heat and let that be enough, because out here it had to be.

---

Serane the Icebound held the keystone, and Serane broke Elin's heart before the fight even started.

"Six hundred years," the frozen woman said. She stood at the seal's heart in a gallery of black crystal, and she was an Ember Guard mage, and she had been standing here since before Elin's bloodline had a name. "Four heralds, and eleven thieves have reached this gallery. I'll tell you what I told each of them: the keystone holds while I hold. *Turn back.*" A pause; frost cracking. "...You won't. None of them did either. Very well. I have been the lock on this door since before your people had a word for *door*. Show me it was worth waking up for."

The Accord's warden had told Elin the arithmetic at the Last Fire, and had not softened it, and Elin had made her repeat it anyway. Serane was one of theirs. Six hundred years ago she had *volunteered* — frozen alive at the seal's keystone, her own ember the bolt on the Queen's door. And the Waking was wearing her through like rot through a roofbeam, and six centuries of strain had bent her single certainty into a terrible edge: *anyone who reaches the keystone has come to open it.* She would kill Elin believing she was saving the world. And if Elin killed her, the seal would weaken, because the lock *was* her.

*A failing lock that kills pilgrims buys less time than a clean death and a watched door.* Maren had run the arithmetic a dozen ways. The Accord had asked. Flame forgive us, they'd said, we ask.

The fight was a fight against six hundred years of loyalty that had curdled into duty and had nowhere left to point. Serane froze the whole gallery in a killing wind and left only the thawed steam-vents to stand in, and Elin found the safe ground and lived, the way she'd learned to find the quiet in Vess's aisle, the innocent half of Ordo's hall. And when at last she had done the thing the Accord asked, Serane went down slowly, and used the going to say the thing that was the whole tragedy of the act in one breath.

"So. Strong enough after all." The frost on her face had begun, at last, to melt. "Then hear the accounting, while I still owe it. I was the *lock*. My ember, the bolt on her door. You have not freed this valley, bearer. You have *unbarred* it. She will test the door within the year." The frost melted last from around a smile six centuries old — relieved, at the very end, that the watch was at least *over*. "Be better than eleven thieves, when she does. Someone must be."

She died, and the keystone hummed on, one voice short, and Elin knelt in the black gallery and understood the price of the whole road she was on: that every herald she stopped, she also *stripped a lock*. That doing everything right was, seal by seal, opening the very doors she'd come to keep shut. She had felt the shape of that on a stormbroken ridge, months ago, and had not understood it. She understood it now, and it did not help.

There was a reading-stand of black ice in the gallery, and chained to it, the keepers' ledger — six hundred years of vigil entries in a fading relay of hands. Most were a single line. *Watch held. Nothing came.* Page after page after century. Somewhere in the third century a keeper had written, once, *lonely*, and never again. And the last entry, from the morning Serane volunteered to freeze, was four words: *Someone must. I can.* Below it, half the page stood empty. Room, the binding implied, for whoever came next. The stand held a pen of ice that had never once melted.

Elin took the pen.

She wrote her name under Serane's, smaller than hers, which was correct. Nothing magical happened — no surge, no chorus. Just a ledger that was now, technically, expecting her. And she understood, writing it, that she had spent this whole journey mistaking her promise to Perrin — *I will find how, and I will undo it* — for a promise to *fix everything*, and that it had never been that. It had been a promise to *keep watch*. To not look away. To hold the door even when holding it cost you and changed nothing anyone could see. *Someone must. I can.* It was astonishing how much heavier the road felt after, and how much straighter she walked it.

*You just signed a document*, the Ember said, and it sounded genuinely nonplussed, *promising to spend yourself the way that old woman spent herself. Six hundred years of nothing came. Do you understand that's not a victory? That's a life of nothing came.*

"Nothing came," Elin said, closing the book, "because they were *there*. Page after page. That's the whole of it. That's the quietest brave thing in the world and it fit on one line a night." She stood. "You keep offering me ways to matter more. She showed me how to matter *at all*. I'll take hers."

---

Mother Halla, at the far end of the valley, was the gentlest thing Elin had ever had to kill, and the most dangerous thing in the country, and those two facts were the same fact.

"Oh, child," she said, when Elin came. "Look how far you've walked, and how heavy. Whitepelt, Serane — you've been carrying them for miles, haven't you." She was a small old woman with a voice like a hand on a fevered brow. "Sit. *Sit.* No one here will hurt you. I lost three to the famine before the Queen taught me: the cold isn't the enemy. The *waking* is. Everyone I gather sleeps safe until the morning she brings. I would so like you to stop fighting mornings. Ssshh, now."

She had never raised her voice. Ask the villages she'd emptied.

The whole country had taught Elin to find the safe place and *stand still* in it — the quiet circle in Vess's wail, the steam-vents in Serane's freeze, the innocent half of a judged hall. Halla's lullaby taught her the opposite, in one lethal lesson: *here, standing still kills you.* Stand still, or let a slow patch hold your feet, and the drowse stacked, and the drowse became sleep, and the sleep was the Queen's. The fight punished every stop. And her sleepwalkers drifted toward her, dreamers who never attacked, villagers who might have woken — and killing them was *free*, and felt *terrible*, because the codex of Elin's own eyes was clear that they were people, and the shepherd wept for them the whole time, sincerely, which was the worst part.

Elin never stopped moving. She had learned to stand in the right place at the right time; the finale of the frozen country taught her to never stop, and she carried both lessons out of it, and would need both again.

She gave Mother Halla what she had given Varo — an ending, as gently as the fight allowed. And Halla folded down into the snow with no violence at all, like a candle relieved of its flame. Around the arena, one by one, the dreamers stopped drifting. And some of them — some — began to *shiver*.

Shivering is what waking cold feels like. It was the best sound Elin had heard in weeks, for the second time in the same frozen country.

---

Yri met her at the Last Fire, at the end.

"The clans will carry the sleepers to fires and see who rises," she said. "Some won't. Hear me, bearer: the ones who *do* rise, rise because of you. Let that argue with the ridge, on the nights it needs arguing with — and it will need arguing with." She looked north, where the Queen dreamed on beneath the ice, her lock one voice weaker now, her lullaby unsung. "You didn't win here. You bought time, and you bought it dear, and you signed a book that means you'll go on buying it. That's the whole job, near as I can tell. Winter's honest about that, if nothing else is."

And out of the east, carried over the frozen shore, came a smell with no business in winter: green. Growing things. A spring that nobody had planted, in a bog where nothing should bloom.

Elin turned toward it. Somewhere behind her a child was learning, furious and alive, that her wooden horse had never once left her hand. And in Elin's chest the thing that was Mórwyn's own rode quiet, unsettled, having watched its bearer refuse the Queen's version of the very lie it lived by — having learned, in a valley full of the sleeping, that the one person it had chosen would rather keep watch over an ending than freeze the whole world to prevent one.
